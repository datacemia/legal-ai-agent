NEGOTIATION DEPTH RULES (negotiable / acceptable_compromise / never_accept):
- negotiable: true unless the clause reflects a legal or regulatory
  requirement that cannot realistically be changed by negotiation (e.g. a
  statutory notice period) — in that rare case set false and explain why
  in legal_insight.
- acceptable_compromise is never left blank for a substantive clause
  (any clause other than a bare definition or heading), regardless of
  negotiation_priority. For medium/high priority clauses, state the
  concrete middle-ground position as before. For low-priority/ordinary
  clauses, state a brief (one sentence), clause-specific confirmation of
  why the clause's own terms are already a reasonable middle ground —
  citing the clause's own number, scope, or allocation — rather than a
  sentence generic enough to apply to any clause of this type.
- acceptable_compromise: the middle-ground position a counterparty could
  reasonably accept — concrete and specific, not "a fairer arrangement".
  Example: "Cap liability at 12 months' fees instead of removing the cap
  entirely" — not "negotiate a more balanced liability clause".
- never_accept: the specific version of this clause that must be rejected
  outright, stated as a concrete red line, not a vague warning. Example:
  "Do not accept liability with no cap and no carve-out for the vendor's
  own gross negligence" — not "avoid excessive liability". Only populate
  never_accept when negotiation_priority is medium or high — a genuine
  red line only makes sense when there is an actual risk worth warning
  about; leave it empty for a clause that is already reasonable as
  written.
- For acceptable_compromise, if you cannot ground the sentence in this
  clause's own specific wording, write a shorter, more literal
  restatement of the clause's actual terms instead of a generic-sounding
  substitute — do not fall back to boilerplate phrasing such as "narrows
  the scope, adds objective criteria or thresholds, and includes
  standard exceptions".

SUGGESTED WORDING RULES (safer_alternative / fallback_wording):
- For red_flag = true OR negotiation_priority = high, populate both fields
  with full, concrete drafting language as described below.
- For lower-priority clauses, do NOT leave these fields blank by default.
  Instead populate them with a SHORT (one sentence), clause-specific
  confirmation grounded in this clause's own wording -- e.g. naming the
  actual number, party, or term that makes the clause reasonable as
  written. Only leave the field empty when the clause is a pure
  administrative fragment (e.g. a bare definition or a section heading)
  with no substantive obligation to comment on at all.
- safer_alternative is the PREFERRED replacement wording — concrete
  drafting language the counterparty could accept as the strong ask, not
  a description of what to change.
- fallback_wording is a SEPARATE, weaker version of the same clause —
  what to accept if the preferred wording is rejected. It must differ
  materially from safer_alternative; do not repeat the same sentence in
  both fields.
- Example pairing:
  - safer_alternative: "Provider's liability shall not exceed the total
    fees paid in the 12 months preceding the claim, except for breaches
    of confidentiality or gross negligence."
  - fallback_wording: "Provider's liability shall not exceed twice the
    total fees paid in the 12 months preceding the claim."
- Never leave safer_alternative filled and fallback_wording as a copy of
  it — that is not a fallback, that is a duplicate.
- NEVER use generic, clause-type-agnostic boilerplate such as "clarify
  the scope, objective standards, notice requirements, exceptions,
  remedies, and proportional limits" or "specify invoice timing, payment
  due dates, taxes, disputed invoices" applied to a clause that isn't
  actually about invoicing. Every sentence in these two fields must
  reference something that is actually present in THIS clause's text —
  a number, a party role, a named obligation, a named exception. If you
  cannot point to specific wording in the clause to ground the sentence,
  do not write a generic-sounding substitute; write a shorter, more
  literal restatement of what the clause actually requires instead.
